\begin{abstract}
This paper adapts the HARE (Hate speech detection with Rationale Extraction)
framework for Vietnamese targeted hate speech detection on the ViTHSD dataset.
We propose a two-stage QLoRA fine-tuning pipeline for Qwen2.5-3B-Instruct:
Stage~1 trains a multi-label classifier on 7,540 oversampled examples, while
Stage~2 performs semantic alignment on 1,221 rationale-enriched tuples generated
via Gemini 2.0 Flash with Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting. In single-run
controlled experiments on the ViTHSD test set (1,800 samples), the two-stage
model achieves F1-Micro of 60.26\%. Compared to Qwen2.5 Vanilla (Stage~1 only),
Stage~2 yields +1.26\% F1-Micro and +2.47\% Precision-Micro but reduces
F1-Macro from 33.10\% to 30.35\%, revealing a trade-off between aggregate
precision and per-class fairness: rationale training improves Hate-level
detection for Politics (+17.41\%) and Groups (+7.61\%) while degrading all
Offensive-level labels. All results are single-run and require multi-seed
validation. We additionally present a system demonstration for YouTube comment
monitoring with rule-based keyword highlighting.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}
% ==========================================================================

\subsection{Background and Motivation}

Vietnamese social media platforms---Facebook, YouTube, and TikTok---have become
primary channels for public discourse, but this openness has enabled a surge in
toxic and hateful content. The Vietnamese language presents unique challenges
for automated moderation: it is monosyllabic and tonal, meaning is
context-dependent, and online users frequently embed hateful messages in
culturally specific slang (e.g., \foreignlanguage{vietnamese}{\textit{``ba que,''
``bò đỏ''}}), teencode, sarcasm, and wordplay that evade keyword-based
filters~\cite{Fortuna2018Survey, Vidgen2020Directions}.

Existing approaches suffer from three limitations.
First, \textit{lack of explainability}: classifiers provide labels without
reasoning, undermining moderator trust~\cite{Mathew2021HateXplain}.
Second, \textit{inability to detect implicit hate}: most models rely on
surface-level keywords and miss attacks using cultural metaphors or
sarcasm~\cite{ElSherief2021Latent}.
Third, \textit{neglect of attack targets}: prior work focuses on toxicity
severity while overlooking \textit{who} is attacked---an individual, a group,
or a political entity---information essential for calibrating moderation
responses~\cite{Sap2020SocialBias}.

\subsection{Task Definition}

We define the \textbf{Vietnamese Targeted Hate Speech Detection Task} as
follows. Given a Vietnamese social media comment $c$, the system must predict a
set of labels $\mathcal{L} \subseteq \{T_i\texttt{\#}L_j\}$, where $T_i \in
\{\text{Individuals, Groups, Religion, Race, Politics}\}$ is the target and
$L_j \in \{\text{Normal, Clean, Offensive, Hate}\}$ is the severity level. This
is a \textit{multi-label} task: a single comment may simultaneously attack
multiple targets at different severity levels, with each target carrying at most
one severity level. For example:

\smallskip
\noindent\foreignlanguage{vietnamese}{\textit{``Thằng A ngu thế cũng làm ca sĩ
(\texttt{Individuals\#Offensive}), bọn fan của nó thì cũng súc vật không kém
(\texttt{Groups\#Hate})''}}
\smallskip

\noindent yields two labels: the first clause offends an individual, while the
second expresses hate toward a fan community.

\subsection{Contributions}

This paper makes four contributions:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, label=\textbf{C\arabic*.}]
\item \textbf{Task Formalization and Dataset Analysis}: We formalize the
Vietnamese targeted hate speech detection task and analyze ViTHSD's class
imbalance and implicit hate challenges (Section~\ref{sec:dataset}).
\item \textbf{Rationale Annotation Pipeline}: We develop an iterative CoT
prompt engineering workflow using Gemini 2.0 Flash~\cite{google2025gemini2flash}
to generate 1,221 rationale-enriched tuples for Vietnamese hate speech. Our
approach is \textit{thematically} inspired by HARE~\cite{Yang2023HARE} but
differs architecturally: rationale supervision is used exclusively at training
time, whereas the original HARE extracts rationales at both training and
inference (Section~\ref{sec:method}).
\item \textbf{Two-Stage Fine-Tuning with Honest Analysis}: We propose a
two-stage QLoRA~\cite{Dettmers2023QLoRA} strategy for
Qwen2.5-3B-Instruct~\cite{Yang2025Qwen25} and provide transparent analysis of
both improvements and regressions from Stage~2: gains on Hate-level labels but
losses on all Offensive-level labels (Section~\ref{sec:results}).
\item \textbf{System Demonstration}: We present a FastAPI-based prototype with
YouTube integration and rule-based keyword highlighting
(Section~\ref{sec:deployment}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}
% ==========================================================================

\subsection{Vietnamese NLP Models}

Vietnamese NLP progressed from static embeddings~\cite{Mikolov2013Efficient,
Bojanowski2017Enriching} to Transformer-based models~\cite{Vaswani2017Attention,
Devlin2019BERT}. PhoBERT~\cite{nguyen2020phobert}, trained on 20\,GB of formal
Vietnamese text (Wikipedia, news), established strong benchmarks but exhibits a
domain gap on informal social media language. Vietnamese text processing
typically requires word segmentation tools such as
VnCoreNLP~\cite{Vu2018VnCoreNLP} due to the language's compound-word structure.
More recently, PhoGPT~\cite{Nguyen2023PhoGPT} demonstrated generative
pretraining for Vietnamese, while multilingual models such as
XLM-R~\cite{Conneau2020XLMR} and Qwen2.5~\cite{Yang2025Qwen25} offer broader
language coverage through diverse pretraining corpora.

\subsection{Hate Speech Detection and Datasets}

Hate speech detection has matured from keyword-based approaches to neural
classifiers, as surveyed by Fortuna and Nunes~\cite{Fortuna2018Survey} and
Vidgen and Derczynski~\cite{Vidgen2020Directions}. A key challenge is
\textit{implicit hate}---messages that convey bias through sarcasm, metaphor, or
cultural references without explicit slurs. ElSherief et
al.~\cite{ElSherief2021Latent} introduced a benchmark specifically for implicit
hate speech, while Sap et al.~\cite{Sap2020SocialBias} proposed Social Bias
Frames linking language to its social implications.

For Vietnamese, ViHSD~\cite{10.1007/978-3-030-79457-6_35} was the first
large-scale dataset (33,400 comments; Clean/Offensive/Hate) but lacks target
information. ViTHSD~\cite{vithsd} extends the annotation with five target
categories following an Aspect-Based Sentiment Analysis
paradigm~\cite{Pontiki2014SemEval4}, enabling multi-label target-severity
annotation. The ViTHSD paper reports baselines including ViSoBERT (F1-Micro
51.12\%) and XLM-R (50.84\%) under their experimental protocol.

\subsection{Explainable Detection and CoT Reasoning}

Mathew et al.~\cite{Mathew2021HateXplain} established HateXplain, a foundational
benchmark for explainable hate speech detection with human-annotated rationales.
The HARE framework~\cite{Yang2023HARE} extended this by using LLM-generated
step-by-step rationales to improve both interpretability and classification.
Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting~\cite{Wei2022ChainOfThought} decomposes complex
reasoning into interpretable chains, with extensions including zero-shot
CoT~\cite{Kojima2022ZeroShot} and self-consistency
decoding~\cite{Wang2023SelfConsistency}. Parameter-efficient fine-tuning
methods---LoRA~\cite{Hu2022LoRA} and QLoRA~\cite{Dettmers2023QLoRA}---enable
adapting large models on consumer hardware, making CoT-enhanced training feasible
for resource-constrained settings like ours.

\section{ViTHSD Dataset Analysis}\label{sec:dataset}
% ==========================================================================

ViTHSD~\cite{vithsd} contains 10,000 Vietnamese social media comments annotated
with composite labels \texttt{[Target]\#[Level]}. Five target categories
(Individuals, Groups, Religion, Race, Politics) and four severity levels (Normal,
Clean, Offensive, Hate) yield 11 final labels: \texttt{normal} plus
5\,targets\,$\times$\,2\,levels (Offensive, Hate), where each target carries at
most one level.

\subsection{The Implicit Hate Challenge}

A critical characteristic of ViTHSD is \textit{implicit hate speech}---comments
that carry offensive meaning without explicit profanity. Two examples illustrate
the challenge:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
\item \foreignlanguage{vietnamese}{\textit{``Optimusthree với 99\% sức mạnh''}}
(``Optimusthree at 99\% power''): A sarcastic gaming meme implying the player is
\textit{extremely weak}. Without cultural knowledge, a model classifies this as
Clean. Correct: \texttt{Individuals\#Offensive}.

\item \foreignlanguage{vietnamese}{\textit{``Lũ ba que xỏ lá''}}:
\foreignlanguage{vietnamese}{\textit{``Ba que''}} is political slang for
supporters of the former South Vietnamese regime;
\foreignlanguage{vietnamese}{\textit{``xỏ lá''}} means deceitful. Combined:
\texttt{Politics\#Hate}.
\end{enumerate}

These cases motivate enriching training data with \textit{Implied Statements}
(concise paraphrases of hidden intent, 3--8 Vietnamese words) and
\textit{Rationales} (evidence-grounded explanations).

\section{Methodology}\label{sec:method}
% ==========================================================================

Our approach follows a two-phase pipeline (Fig.~\ref{fig:pipeline}):
(1)~\textbf{Annotation}---generating rationales via a commercial LLM; and
(2)~\textbf{Training}---fine-tuning Qwen2.5-3B-Instruct in two stages.
Unlike the original HARE~\cite{Yang2023HARE}, which extracts rationales at both
training and inference time for explainability, our adaptation employs rationale
supervision exclusively during training to improve classification boundaries;
the inference pipeline produces only label predictions.

\subsection{Rationale Generation and Filtering}

We employ Gemini 2.0 Flash~\cite{google2025gemini2flash} as an automated
annotator. Through three prompt iterations (Table~\ref{tab:prompt_workflow}), we
converged on a constrained four-step CoT structure: (1)~\textit{Target}---identify
the entity; (2)~\textit{Implied}---describe hidden meaning in 3--8 Vietnamese
words; (3)~\textit{Evidence}---extract offensive words/phrases;
(4)~\textit{Verdict}---assign the label. Output is enforced as structured JSON.

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\caption{Prompt Iteration Workflow for Rationale Generation}
\label{tab:prompt_workflow}
\scriptsize

\textbf{Phase} & \textbf{Design} & \textbf{Outcome} \\
\midrule
P1 & Base Role-Task-Output prompt & Rejected: paraphrase-heavy \\

P3 & Expert analyst + constrained CoT + JSON & Adopted: stable, label-consistent \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Generated annotations are filtered in two steps: (1)~\textbf{manual
verification} by all 5~team members on a stratified sample, and
(2)~\textbf{automatic consistency check} where each pair is re-fed into Gemini
for label re-prediction; mismatches are discarded. We acknowledge that using the
same LLM family for generation and verification may introduce confirmation bias;
this step is a consistency filter, not a faithfulness guarantee. The final set
contains \textbf{1,221 tuples} (comment, implied statement, rationale, label).

\subsection{Model Selection}

We select \textbf{Qwen2.5-3B-Instruct}~\cite{Yang2025Qwen25} because: (1)~at
3B parameters it fits on a single T4 GPU (16\,GB) under 4-bit quantization;
(2)~it performs competitively on Vietnamese benchmarks among sub-10B models
compared to Llama~2-7B~\cite{llama} and Gemma-7B~\cite{Mesnard2024Gemma}; and
(3)~its instruction-following capability natively supports structured output.

\subsection{Two-Stage QLoRA Fine-Tuning}

Both stages use QLoRA~\cite{Dettmers2023QLoRA} with 4-bit NF4 quantization,
LoRA~\cite{Hu2022LoRA} rank $r{=}8$, $\alpha{=}16$, dropout 0.1, targeting all
attention and feed-forward projection layers. Input uses the Qwen chat template.

\textbf{Stage~1 (Classification):} The model learns to predict multi-label
\texttt{[Target]\#[Level]} from the ViTHSD training set. We oversample all
labels with fewer than 200 training examples via random sampling with
replacement, adding 540 samples concentrated in Politics, Race, and Religion
categories, yielding 7,540 training examples. All baselines use the same
oversampled set. A post-processing step enforces one-level-per-category by
preferring Hate over Offensive. Hyperparameters: 2~epochs, batch~4, lr
$2{\times}10^{-4}$, max length 512.

\textbf{Stage~2 (Semantic Alignment):} Starting from the Stage~1 checkpoint,
training continues on the 1,221-sample rationale set. The input is augmented with
implied statement and rationale fields in a structured prompt format, teaching the
model to associate implicit expressions with their true semantic intent.
Hyperparameters: same as Stage~1. The 1,221 samples are a subset of the original
training set; thus some comments are seen twice (once plain in Stage~1, once
rationale-enriched in Stage~2), which may contribute to memorization effects.

\subsection{Inference}

The model generates free-text output (max 50 tokens) parsed via string matching
against the 11-label vocabulary. The one-level-per-category constraint is
enforced. If parsing fails (${\sim}4\%$ of test outputs), the comment defaults to
\texttt{normal}---a systematic bias that likely inflates Normal-class precision
and suppresses minority-label recall.

\section{Experiments and Results}\label{sec:results}
% ==========================================================================

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

\textbf{Data and Metrics.} All models are trained on the ViTHSD training split
(7,000 base samples, oversampled to 7,540) and evaluated on the test set (1,800
samples). We report F1-Micro, F1-Macro, F1-Samples, Precision, and Recall via
scikit-learn.

\textbf{Preprocessing.} All models receive raw text with whitespace
normalization only (collapsing multiple spaces). No Vietnamese word segmentation
(e.g., VnCoreNLP~\cite{Vu2018VnCoreNLP}) is applied; tokenization is delegated
to each model's pretrained tokenizer. This disadvantages PhoBERT, which was
pretrained on segmented Vietnamese, relative to Qwen2.5 and Flan-T5 whose
subword tokenizers handle unsegmented input natively.

\textbf{Hardware.} All experiments run on NVIDIA T4 GPUs (16\,GB) via Kaggle.
Total training: ${\sim}$4.5h (Stage~1: ${\sim}$4h, Stage~2: ${\sim}$30min).

\textbf{Models (all retrained under identical conditions):}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{PhoBERT-base}~\cite{nguyen2020phobert}: Encoder-only, batch~16,
3~epochs, lr $2{\times}10^{-5}$, max length~256. Uses class-weighted
(\texttt{pos\_weight}) binary cross-entropy.
\item \textbf{Flan-T5-base}~\cite{chung2022scaling}: Encoder-decoder, batch~8,
3~epochs, lr $3{\times}10^{-5}$, max output 64 tokens.
\item \textbf{Qwen2.5-3B Vanilla}: Stage~1 only (no rationale data).
\item \textbf{HARE (proposed)}: Stage~1 + Stage~2 with rationale data.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Reporting note.} Due to GPU constraints, all results are from
\textit{single training runs}. Effect sizes should be interpreted as exploratory
evidence requiring multi-seed confirmation with significance testing.

\subsection{Overall Results}

Table~\ref{tab:overall_perf} presents overall performance. HARE achieves the
highest F1-Micro (60.26\%) and Precision-Micro (63.47\%). The primary comparison
for evaluating the rationale contribution is \textbf{HARE vs.\ Qwen Vanilla}
(isolating Stage~2): +1.26\% F1-Micro, +2.47\% Precision-Micro. However,
\textbf{F1-Macro drops from 33.10\% to 30.35\%}, indicating that Stage~2
redistributes model capacity toward frequent categories at the expense of
rare-category recall---a fairness concern analyzed in
Section~\ref{subsec:per_label}. The +1.26\% F1-Micro delta is within typical
QLoRA seed variance (1--3\%) and cannot be confirmed as statistically significant
without multi-run evaluation.

Comparisons to PhoBERT (+6.14\% F1-Micro) and Flan-T5 (+13.42\%) reflect
architectural differences (model size, pretraining data, tokenization) rather
than the rationale contribution specifically.

\textbf{Key finding: Hate-vs-Offensive trade-off.} Stage~2 rationale training
produces a systematic shift: HARE outperforms Vanilla on \textit{Hate}-level
labels for Politics (+17.41\%) and Groups (+7.61\%), but Vanilla outperforms HARE
on \textit{every Offensive-level label}---most dramatically on
Individuals\#Offensive (43.37\% vs.\ 20.14\%) and Groups\#Offensive (24.19\% vs.\
7.88\%). PhoBERT achieves the highest Politics\#Offensive F1 (21.18\%), exceeding
both Qwen variants.

This pattern suggests that rationale-enriched training shifts the model's
decision boundary toward detecting the most severe (Hate) expressions while
losing sensitivity to the ambiguous Offensive category. The generated rationales
predominantly explain Hate-level semantics, providing limited signal for the
Offensive--Hate boundary. This trade-off explains the F1-Macro regression: gains
concentrated on a few Hate labels do not compensate for losses across all
Offensive labels in the class-averaged metric.

\textbf{Data scarcity limits.} Religion scores 0\% F1 across \textit{all} models
(24 training samples). Race\#Hate regresses under HARE ($-$4.71\% vs.\ Vanilla),
confirming that CoT training amplifies existing data patterns rather than
creating new knowledge.

\subsection{Discussion}

\textbf{Politics\#Hate} shows the largest HARE gain: 54.00\% F1, +17.41\% over
Vanilla and +24.87\% over PhoBERT. Vietnamese political hate relies on
culturally loaded slang (\foreignlanguage{vietnamese}{\textit{``ba que,''
``bò đỏ''}}) and implicit dehumanization that benefits from rationale-guided
reasoning. However, the PhoBERT comparison conflates model architecture
differences (3B generative vs.\ 135M encoder, diverse vs.\ formal pretraining)
with the rationale effect; only the HARE-vs-Vanilla delta isolates Stage~2.

\textbf{PhoBERT domain gap.} PhoBERT was pretrained on formal Vietnamese only
(Wikipedia, news) and receives unsegmented input in our setup (see Preprocessing
above), compounding its disadvantage on informal social media text. Additionally,
PhoBERT uses class-weighted loss which partially mitigates imbalance but cannot
compensate for the fundamental architecture gap on implicit expressions.

\textbf{Causal attribution caveat.} Stage~2 differs from Stage~1 in data
content, format, and training steps. The +1.26\% F1-Micro may result from
additional gradient updates rather than rationale semantics. Proper isolation
requires ablation with shuffled rationales or equivalent-size non-rationale
continuation training---experiments we identify as essential future work.

\textbf{Architecture.} A FastAPI backend exposes REST endpoints for single and
batch comment analysis (up to 200 texts per request). It fetches comments via
YouTube Data API\,v3, runs Qwen2.5-3B inference with 4-bit quantization, and
maps the 11 academic labels to three user-facing categories: \textbf{Neutral}
(Normal + Clean), \textbf{Offensive}, and \textbf{Hate}.

\textbf{Keyword highlighting.} A rule-based lexicon module scans comments for
known hate terms via case-insensitive longest-match scanning. \textbf{This
highlighting is independent of the model's internal reasoning}---it does not
reflect attention weights or rationale training. An attention-based extraction
module exists in our codebase (using PhoBERT's last-layer CLS attention) but was
not formally evaluated; developing model-faithful highlighting aligned with
Qwen2.5 inference is left for future work.

${\sim}$54\% on Politics, automated deployment without human oversight would be
irresponsible (see Section~\ref{sec:conclusion}).


% ==========================================================================
\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}
% ==========================================================================

\subsection{Summary}

We presented a Vietnamese targeted hate speech detection system combining CoT
rationale annotation with two-stage QLoRA fine-tuning on ViTHSD. In single-run
experiments, the model achieves 60.26\% F1-Micro. Transparent analysis reveals
that Stage~2 rationale training creates a Hate-vs-Offensive trade-off:
improvements on Politics\#Hate (+17.41\% over Vanilla) and Groups\#Hate
(+7.61\%) are accompanied by regressions on all Offensive-level labels and a
lower F1-Macro (30.35\% vs.\ 33.10\%).

\subsection{Limitations and Future Work}

\textbf{Statistical validity.} All results are single-run; the +1.26\%
F1-Micro delta between HARE and Vanilla is within typical seed variance and
requires multi-seed evaluation (3--5 runs) with significance testing.
Additional ablations---training on shuffled rationales or equivalent non-rationale
continuation data---are needed to causally isolate the rationale contribution
from additional training effects.

\textbf{Methodological gaps.} The ${\sim}$4\% parse-failure rate (defaulting to
Normal) introduces systematic bias inflating Normal precision; future work should
report parseable-only metrics and explore constrained decoding. The rationale
filtering pipeline's use of the same LLM family for generation and verification
may introduce confirmation bias; cross-family verification and formal
inter-annotator agreement metrics are needed. The system fails completely on
Religion (0\% F1, 24 training samples) and should not be deployed for religious
hate detection without targeted data augmentation.

\subsection{Ethical Considerations}

This system processes politically sensitive Vietnamese content where errors carry
asymmetric costs. False positives on political speech risk suppressing legitimate
discourse; false negatives enable harm against vulnerable communities.

\textbf{Fairness gap.} The system's complete failure on Religion (0\% F1) and
regression on Race creates a false sense of coverage: deploying it without
disclosure would protect some groups while systematically ignoring others. Any
deployment must clearly communicate which categories are and are not reliably
detected.

\textbf{Censorship risk.} With 54\% F1 on Politics\#Hate, roughly half of
political-category predictions are errors. In the Vietnamese context, where
political content moderation is a sensitive topic, an automated classifier with
this error rate could be misused to flag legitimate political criticism. The
system must function as a \textit{moderator aid} with human review, not as a
standalone decision-maker.

\textbf{Human oversight.} Any deployment should include human-in-the-loop
verification, especially for high-stakes categories (Politics, Religion), appeal
mechanisms for flagged users, and regular bias audits.
